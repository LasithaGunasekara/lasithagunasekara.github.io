Current Research

My current research focuses on the behavior of active nematics confined to the surface of a viscous drop suspended in an external shear flow. This system provides a rich platform to study the coupling between interfacial active stresses and background hydrodynamics.

Specifically, I aim to understand how the drop deforms in response to both the internal active stresses generated by the surface-confined nematic layer and the external shear forces. The presence of topological defects where the nematic orientation is singular plays a crucial role in the system's evolution. I study how these defects emerge, move, interact, and undergo braiding dynamics as the system evolves over time.

Another important direction of this work is to explore how the initial configuration of defects influences the long-time behavior of the system. In future stages of the project, we plan to examine the role of coupled nematic layers inside and outside the drop, and how this affects the morphology and defect dynamics of the interface.

This research sits at the intersection of soft matter physics, fluid dynamics, and geometry, and has potential implications for understanding biological systems and designing synthetic active materials.

×

Topic: Survey on Suspensions of Particles in Viscous (Stokesian) Fluids

Abstract:

This study focuses on the dynamics of capsules suspended in small-scale, slow-moving fluids (Stokes flow). We consider both deformable capsules (red blood cells, vesicles, drops, and bubbles) and rigid bodies in flows where the fluidâ€™s viscous forces are much larger than its inertial forces. Deformable capsules interact with the fluid through forces like bending, repulsion, adhesion, stretching, and tension, creating a strong two-way coupling between the capsule shape and the fluid dynamics. Simulating these systems presents several challenges. One is resolving close interactions between membranes or with solid walls. Another is developing time stepping methods that minimize numerical stiffness that arises from simulating deformable membranes, particularly when dealing with large deformations. Simulating large systems also requires scalable methods, including parallel computing, HPC acceleration, and fast algorithms such as the Fast Multipole Method. We review key numerical methods including Boundary Integral Equation methods, Phase-Field models, Finite Element Methods, and Lattice Boltzmann Methods. Each method offers different advantages in terms of accuracy, stability, and computational cost. This survey helps guide the selection of appropriate strategies for modeling complex suspensions in low-Reynolds number flows, with relevance to biomedical and microfluidic applications.

The role of shear on an active nematic viscous drop

×

Topic: The role of shear on an active nematic viscous drop

Abstract:

Interfaces suspended in a viscous solvent often include in-plane activity along the interface, which couples to the hydrodynamics. Recent work by M. Firouznia and D. Saintillan consider the dynamics of a viscous drop with interfacial nematic activity. Using a spectral representation for the drop and a spectral boundary integral equation method to resolve the hydrodynamics, they investigate the role of the active capillary number and the active Peclet number. However, they do not consider the role of a background flow. In this poster, I consider the dynamics of an active nematic viscous drop suspended in a shear flow. This serves as a first step toward simulating suspensions of active nematic interfaces within confining geometries.

Project 1: Survey and Analysis of Methods for Near-Singular Integration

Course Project â€“ Near Singular Integration (Spring 2025)
Team: Lasitha Gunasekara, Sanjida Yeasmin, Jonathan Engle
Advisor: Dr. Bryan Quaife

Department: Department Of Scientific Computing

This project focused on the numerical challenges involved in evaluating near-singular integrals, which commonly arise in boundary integral methods used in fluid dynamics, electromagnetics, and elasticity. Standard quadrature techniques often fail when the evaluation point lies close to the boundary, due to steep gradients in the integrand. Our goal was to survey, analyze, and compare modern numerical strategies designed to address these difficulties.

We reviewed several key approaches, including extrapolated regularization by Beale and Tlupova (2024), which replaces singular kernels with smooth approximations, and Quadrature by Expansion (QBX) by KlÃ¶ckner et al. (2013), which evaluates layer potentials using local series expansions near the boundary. We also examined the differential geometric method introduced by Zhu and Veerapaneni (2022), which reformulates layer potentials using exterior calculus and quaternionic forms, achieving high-order accuracy. Another core technique studied was the three-region method proposed by Biros, Ying, and Zorin (2006), which partitions the domain based on proximity to the boundary and applies different quadrature strategies accordingly.

We implemented and tested several of these methods, compared their convergence behavior, and evaluated their computational efficiency. The final report presents both the theoretical insights and numerical results, serving as a foundation for future work in this area.

Project 2: Modeling and Analysis of Spin Coating

Course Project â€“ Advanced Transport Phenomena (Fall 2024)
Team: Lasitha Gunasekara, Isaac, and Maureen
Advisor: Dr. Subramanian Ramakrishna
Department: FAMU-FSU College of Engineering (Chemical Engineering)

This project focused on the mathematical modeling and physical interpretation of spin coating, a key technique for producing uniform thin polymer films. Using the classic works by Bornside et al. (1991) and Meyerhofer (1978), we derived and analyzed the governing transport equations for film thinning due to both radial flow and solvent evaporation. The objective was to derive the dry film thickness and evaluate the assumptions involved in lubrication theory for high-speed rotating substrates.

We analytically solved the r-momentum Navierâ€“Stokes equation under the thin-film approximation and established pseudosteady conditions. We confirmed that key dimensionless groups such as the Reynolds number and viscous scaling supported the neglect of inertial and radial viscous terms. The derived film thickness equation matched the one proposed by Bornside et al., connecting it back to physical parameters such as spin speed, viscosity, and solvent fraction.

The second part of the project critically reviewed the Bornside model and recreated the original Figure 3 showing dry film thickness versus spin speed for different polymer concentrations. We compared experimental and theoretical data and highlighted limitations due to simplifications, especially the decoupling of evaporation and convective thinning stages.

Project 3: Truncated SVD (tSVD) and Its Applications

Graduate Seminar Presentation â€“ Spring 2024
Course: ISC5934 (Graduate Seminar)
Instructor: Dr. Sachin Shanbhag
Department: Scientific Computing, Florida State University

This individual project focused on exploring the theory and applications of Truncated Singular Value Decomposition (tSVD). The tSVD is a reduced-rank approximation technique derived from the standard SVD, where only the largest k singular values and corresponding vectors are retained. This makes it a powerful tool for dimensionality reduction, noise filtering, and solving ill-conditioned linear systems.

In the presentation, I discussed:

  • The difference between SVD and tSVD with geometric and algebraic interpretations.
  • A hands-on example with a small matrix to show the impact of truncation.
  • Applications of tSVD in scientific computing.
  • A case study where tSVD is used to improve the conditioning of linear systems arising from highly oscillatory integrals, referencing work by Li et al. that utilizes Chebyshev spectral methods and Levin-type quadrature.
